Differences in reporting systems and measured and real incidence of AIDS: Switzerland and the Federal Republic of Germany (FRG).

Estermann JC, Zwahlen M, Kent C, Skarabis H; International Conference on AIDS.

Int Conf AIDS. 1991 Jun 16-21; 7: 370 (abstract no. M.C.3289).

Federal Office of Public Health, Epidemiology Section, Bern, Switzerland

OBJECTIVE: To evaluate the reliability of officially measured AIDS incidence in countries with different reporting systems. METHODS: Data from the AIDS registries of Switzerland and the Federal Republic of Germany (FRG) were compared. Switzerland has a mandatory reporting system for AIDS cases supplemented by active surveillance through death certificates. The FRG has a voluntary reporting system without such a systematic external validation system. Both systems are anonymous. The former German Democratic Republic (GDR) has a non-anonymous mandatory reporting system which was adapted to the FRG's system in spring 1990. RESULTS: The cumulative number of AIDS cases through Dec. 31, 1990 is 1615 in Switzerland and 5612 in the FRG. The cumulative incidence in Switzerland is 24.2 AIDS cases per 100,000 habitants, while in the former GDR it is 0.2 compared to 9.2 in the FRG (boundaries of 1989). During 1990, 459 cases were reported in Switzerland and 1306 cases in the FRG, corresponding to 7.1 and 2.1 AIDS cases per 100,000 habitants respectively. The mean reporting delay was between 4 and 5 months in the FRG, with a similar delay in Switzerland for cases reported spontaneously by physicians (87% of all cases). In 1990, 24% of all cases in Switzerland were found by death certificates. For these cases, the mean reporting delay was 16 months. The completeness of reporting in the FRG is estimated to be between 50 and 85%. In Switzerland, 95% of the deceased patients are estimated to be reported, compared to 65 to 80% among patients not known to be deceased. DISCUSSION AND CONCLUSIONS: Voluntary systems without additional instruments for estimating underreporting may lead to a substantial underestimation of AIDS incidence. However, voluntary reporting systems are not necessarily more incomplete than mandatory systems. Even in industrialized countries, underreporting of AIDS cases may be vary between 50 and 95%, making international comparisons of incidence very difficult. Active surveillance of AIDS cases, validation of the reporting systems, and empirical assessment of underreporting is strongly suggested for all national reporting systems.
