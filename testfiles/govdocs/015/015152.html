Increased adherence of Candida albicans to buccal epithelial cells (BEC) obtained from AIDS patients.

Schwab U, Milatovic D, Braveny I; American Society for Microbiology. General Meeting.

Abstr Gen Meet Am Soc Microbiol. 1995; 95: 95 (abstract no. F-47).

Inst. Med. Microbiol. Technical University, Munich, FRG.

Adherence of candida to mucosal epithelial cells is an important initial step in the process of colonization and subsequently invasion. Because recurrent oral infections with C. albicans are common in patients with AIDS we compared the adherence of C. albicans to the BEC of 2 AIDS patients and 2 volunteers. Six genotypically distinct C. albicans isolates were used: 4 strains were isolated from AIDS patients with oral candidiasis, 1 strain was isolated from the tracheal secretion of an ICU patient and 1 strain from the throat culture of a patient with leukemia. Using interference contrast microscopy, binding of C. albicans to the cells was directly visualized and the mean number of yeast cells attached to each of at least 50 BEC was determined. Adherence tests were performed weekly over a period of 4 weeks. The BEC from the 2 AIDS patients tested showed a 2-5 fold increased binding compared to the BEC from control persons. Comparing the adherence of the 6 C. albicans isolates 5 strains demonstrated remarkably similar binding to the BEC of the same individual, whereas one isolate from an AIDS patient bound in higher numbers to the cells. Increased adherence may not only be associated with differences in epithelial cell receptors in AIDS patients, but also depend on virulence factors of the Candida strain such as adhesins. Our results indicate that increased adherence plays an important role in the pathogenesis of recurrent oral infections in AIDS patients.
