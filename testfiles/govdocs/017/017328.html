The Seasoned Investor: You
The Week in Review

By Jerry Thomas (TMF Cheeze)
June 16, 2000

Greetings, Fools.

What do you invest in?

Or, put another way, what should you invest in?

I'm not going to answer that question. It's not my department, Fool. If you want to make money, my only suggestion is that you take the same path you would follow for any endeavor at which you intended to excel: Study your subject. Steep yourself in its details and its nuances. Develop a sense of mastery over it. And practice, practice, practice, resolving to learn something from every mistake you make along the way. Believe me, you're going to make mistakes. When you have persisted in this discipline for a number of years, you will become a seasoned investor. It's as simple, and as complicated, as that.

The curious result of this is that your version of The Seasoned Investor will probably not look like anyone else's. You will develop your unique investing strategy that will represent your interests, personality, and needs. While all investors aim to make money, each individual's approach to that goal will be his or her own creation. Rule Breaker investors do not behave like Drip investors. Rule Makers and Mechanical Investors think different thoughts altogether. And yet they can all be successful.

Our Boring Portfolio will appeal to one sort of investor, the kind that pays special attention to the price he pays for a stock. He is less interested in the story a company can tell than in the facts and figures of its financial statements. Boring investors are bottom-liners: They care a great deal about the price they pay for each incremental bit of profit a company is expected to generate. Price-to-earnings ratios loom large in this discipline, and Monday's Boring Portfolio report by Warren Gump (TMF Gump) is as good an encapsulation of the Boring approach to investing as you're likely to find.

Rule Maker investors, too, are rabid in their hunger for examining financial statements. Yet their discipline seeks a different emphasis, concerning itself less with P/Es and more on the competitive advantages each company has. Does this business dominate its industry? Does it generate cash in large amounts? Does it have a healthy balance sheet and can investors comfortably expect the company to continue its winning ways for decades to come? In Monday's Rule Maker report, Matt Richey (TMF Verve) reviews the tenets of RM investing by looking at the "Simpleton Portfolio," a list of 10 stocks Fool founder Tom Gardner made five years ago, stocks that he asserted he would feel comfortable holding for a full decade -- even with no trading in and out of those positions for the complete 10-year term.

I won't tell you the return on those 10 stocks here at the midpoint of that experiment, partly because Matt does that himself, but mainly because they are embarrassingly, even unrealistically positive. I don't want to suggest that any investor should expect to see those kinds of returns in her portfolio, because I doubt they can be repeated with any kind of consistency. I do not by any stretch of the imagination want to imply that such dramatic outperformance in the stock market is automatic, or even likely. I do, however, suggest that Rule Maker investing is worth your time to study, and to mention that our next online seminar in this approach will be beginning soon. Learning to achieve even a fraction of this sort of success could be a major step in your advancement toward becoming your version of The Seasoned Investor.

This has been a good week in Fooldom for men, and for women, and for men and women together. First, remember that this Sunday is Father's Day, which means it's time once again for our annual Stocks for Dad feature. Yes, I'm sure your father will love the necktie or the golf clubs or the bottle of Old Spice you'll give him, but he might especially like this list of favorite companies suggested by some of our Fool staff members -- companies they expect will do well in the years to come. You, the future seasoned investor will have to make your own determination whether these companies are worth your investment dollar. Hey, maybe you and Dad can study these companies together. Use this list as a solid idea starter for beginning your own research.

Women should pay special attention to this week's Retiree Portfolios report from Anne Letterese, which she calls a Retirement Wake-Up Call. Because women tend to live longer than men, and because they often face special challenges in the workplace, the importance of retirement planning looms even larger for women than it does for men. Part of being a savvy investor is knowing what you're investing for, and Anne's report will give you some sharp reminders about the urgent need to prepare yourself for your senior years. That, of course, is true for both men and women, but especially for women, given the bald facts of the matter.

For men and women together, Monday's Fribble will tell you how living on a budget can actually be romantic. I suspect that this concept alone is compelling enough to get you to click over to see it without my attempts to entice you any further. Hey, who said Love and Money aren't compatible?

This week Chris Rugaber (TMF RFK) reviews the book Irrational Exuberance, by Yale professor Robert Shiller, who says the market is in an unsustainable speculative phase, boding ill returns for investors in the years to come. I don't care how bullish you might be -- I think it is always prudent for any investor to stop for a moment to think about worst-case scenarios. Like I said, steep yourself in the details and nuances of investing, Fool, and seek opinions from all points on the spectrum of understanding.

But once you're finished with the doom and gloom, take some time out for some entertainment. That's what our Fun & Folly area is for, and there you'll find some jokes, games, and a very silly item I've written entitled What I Learned in Vegas. I expect Cheeze fans might find it amusing. Hey, if you've managed to read this far, I figure I owe you some laughs.

Until next week,
