Independent GAO Testimony Confirms Need for Greater Collaboration Between Head Start, Other Childhood Programs

WASHINGTON, D.C. -- In a hearing yesterday before the Senate Committee on Health, Education, Labor and Pensions (HELP), Marnie S. Shaul, the Director of Education, Workforce, and Income Security Issues for the General Accounting Office (GAO) testified that barriers to collaboration among programs administered by states and Head Start are impeding the effectiveness of all of these programs. The School Readiness Act (H.R. 2210), authored by Education Reform Subcommittee Chairman Mike Castle (R-DE), would address these barriers by allowing states to coordinate federal childcare programs, state-run pre-kindergarten programs and Head Start.

According to Ms. Shaul’s testimony, which is available at http://www.gao.gov/new.items/d03840t.pdf, “barriers sometimes exist, making it difficult to blend services offered across programs to meet the demands of families.” These barriers are exacerbated by the fact that states administer the vast majority of programs that provide early childhood care and education. Even programs funded by the federal government, such as Temporary Assistance for Needy Families (TANF) and the Child Care and Development Fund (CCDF), are administered by the state governments, and are therefore easy to coordinate with state-run preschool programs. Only Head Start lies outside this existing network of collaboration.

House Committee on Education and the Workforce Chairman John Boehner (R-OH), an original cosponsor of the School Readiness Act, said “The GAO testimony today underscores what supporters of Head Start reform have been saying since this bill was first introduced: barriers to coordination and collaboration are preventing disadvantaged children from getting the best this nation has to offer.”

“The School Readiness Act, by increasing collaboration among the various state and federal programs will ensure that the maximum number of children are served by programs that maintain the highest standards,” Boehner continued. “Collaboration will help close the readiness gap between Head Start graduates and their more affluent peers.”

Ms. Shaul also testified that the practical implications of a lack of effective collaboration are very real. She said, “To meet the demands of families, some federal programs require coordination of services among early childhood education and care programs. To illustrate, most Head Start programs are predominately part day, part year programs, and they cannot meet the demands of working families who need full-day, full-year education and care services. In response to this requirement, some Head Start programs collaborate with other programs to provide families full day coverage. However, differing program eligibility requirements and other coordination barriers sometimes impede coordination efforts.” (Emphasis added).

Boehner added, “GAO’s independent testimony demonstrates that a lack of coordination among programs administered by the states and Head Start results in gaps in service, and difficulty providing the full-day, full-year preschool program that many children in single-parent families, or in families with two working parents, require. Ms. Shaul and GAO correctly note that ‘much has changed in society since Head Start was established nearly 40 years ago.’ Unfortunately, not enough has changed in the Head Start program to ensure that all barriers to coordinating state and federal programs are removed. The School Readiness Act would take the first steps towards guaranteeing that all early care and education programs are coordinated at all levels, making certain that the maximum number of children can receive the valuable services that Head Start and other programs have to offer.”

By giving states with a demonstrated commitment to early childhood education the opportunity to coordinate existing pre-school programs and federal child care grants such as TANF and CCDBG with Head Start, the School Readiness Act would eliminate the barriers to coordination that are preventing too many children from getting the quality pre-school program that they deserve. As an earlier HHS report also noted, problems with collaboration can also have a negative effect on school readiness, perpetuating the current readiness gap between Head Start graduates and their more affluent peers.

GAO is the independent audit, evaluation, and investigative arm of Congress, existing to help improve the performance and ensure the accountability of the federal government for the American people.

H.R. 2210 is expected to come before the full House of Representatives for a vote later this week.
