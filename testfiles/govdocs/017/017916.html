Would you like to climb a mountain?

04.30.2007 — On Tuesday, Ruth, one of my braver students, asked me if I would like to climb a mountain with her and her dorm mates this weekend. I told her I thought that it sounded like fun and I gave her my phone number and told her to call me if they made plans. (Partially I thought this was a good idea because I have been in China long enough to know that when my students say they are going to "climb a mountain" they really mean they are going for a hike. It doesn't matter how many times I pantomime climbing a mountain and explain how that is different from hiking, each Monday they still tell me about their weekend mountain climbing! )
 
Sure enough, on Friday night I got a call asking if Thad and I wanted to go. I said we would love to and we would meet them at the school gate at 8AM. (The school gate is the meeting point for EVERYTHING that goes on around here.)
 
On Saturday morning when I woke up, my stomach felt a little rumbly, but I figured it would get better once I was up and about, so we headed out. That may or may not have been a good choice. We had been out walking for about 1/2 an hour when my stomach started doing flips again. We were in the middle of the countryside, walking on a path between farmers' fields. I had no choice but to ask the girls about the possibility of a toilet. That in and of itself is a little embarrassing, but wait, the story gets worse! So we are in the middle of nowhere and two girls haul me off the path to a stand of trees. (Now, keep in mind that rumbly stomach isn't what you want when a stand of trees is your only restroom, but again, no choice...) So we get there, but there are some men farmers hanging around the trees, so we decide that isn't a good option. I told them we should just keep walking on the path and see what we could find. We went back to where Thad and the other girls were waiting (embarrassing!!) and head on down the path. Soon we came to a little village, so one of the girls ran to a house and asked if they could use the bathroom. The woman says yes, but I don't think they mentioned it was for a foreigner, because when I went back with the girl, the woman was more than a little shocked! haha!
 
The bathroom was similar to many that we find in the countryside, which means the toilet is a cinder block "building" with three walls and a small (too small!) sheet that served as a door. Outside the building was a mean dog that was barking and about to break his chain, so I was a little worried about that. I went in and the toilet itself was a cement slot in the ground with a cement ramp leading to a bucket. (Let's just say I have now helped to fertilize some food here in China!) Okay, so I go in and two of the girls stand outside. I was inside and outside was this crazy goose that kept sticking his head in the sheet and honking at me! Weird!!! After a minute or so, one of the girls came to the curtain to make sure I had paper. (I did; we carry it everywhere! It was nice of her to ask though.) After a couple of minutes I finished taking care of business and headed back out. My stomach was feeling much better and we headed on down the path. Over the next ten minutes though, each girl came to me individually to ask how my stomach was. I guess there was no hiding that issue. I told them I was a little embarrassed, but they all said "it is nothing." Haha! Well, that was an adventure! I guess I should just be glad we found somewhere, because it all could have turned out much worse!! Embarrassing, but not the mortifying situation it could have been.
 
The rest of the hike was beautiful. Spring has sprung here in rural Gansu and the farmers are hard at work in their fields. Everything was bursting with color. The peach trees were in blossom with vibrant pink flowers; the pear trees were blooming an innocent white and the all through the fields the green sprouts of various veggies were starting to show through the dirt.
 

 
I love getting out with the students on the weekend because it is a fabulous opportunity for them to practice their English. As we walk, we chat about their boyfriends, college life, what they want to do after they finish school, what their goals for the future are and about every other topic imaginable. They also tend to drill me about American culture and life in the States and want to know everything they can about living abroad. I feel like these weekend get-togethers not only give the students a chance to stretch their English skills, but they teach me massive amounts about Chinese culture and the area in which Thad and I are living for two years. It is a win-win situation!!
