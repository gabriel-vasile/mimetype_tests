Anthony Dominic Abdullah

Consultant geriatrician Central Middlesex Hospital (b Cairns, Australia, 1902; q Sydney 1926; FRCP), d 9 January 1999. It was difficult for overseas doctors to obtain medical posts in teaching hospitals before the war, but Tony was appointed assistant medical officer at the Central Middlesex in 1930 at £400 a year when the hospital was a poor law institution for the indigent sick of Willesden. Together with other physicians he helped upgrade the hospital to a modern teaching hospital. Tony became the first director of the department of geriatrics, and after the war continued to develop a modern geriatric service. In 1942 he was appointed medical specialist and commander of the 151st East African General Hospital, becoming a full colonel. It was staffed by African medical orderlies, whom Tony learnt to address in Swahili. Outside medicine he loved poetry and Latin and was passionate about cricket. He leaves a wife; a son; and two daughters.

Shaukat Hussain Ansari

General practitioner Newcastle and former hospital practitioner in psychiatry South Tyneside District Hospital (b 1945; q Karachi 1969), died from complications after myocardial infarction on 15 October 1998. Shaukat devoted much of his time to the north east branch of the Pakistan Medical Society, of which he was an executive member. Outside medicine he enjoyed music, gardening, sports, particularly cricket, and Urdu poetry; he was a member of Bazm-e-Ehbab of Newcastle. He leaves a wife, Shireen; a daughter; and two sons.

Roland Barnes

Former professor of orthopaedic surgery Glasgow (b 1907; q Manchester 1930; FRCS, FRCSGlas; CBE), d 15 November 1998. His orthopaedic training was extended by a year spent at the Hospital for Special Surgery, New York, and by numerous visits to Europe. At the outbreak of the second world war he was asked to establish a large orthopaedic unit at Warrington with a special interest in peripheral nerve injuries as part of the Emergency Medical Service. In 1942 he moved to Glasgow as the first Frederick Young lecturer in orthopaedics in the department of surgery. Roland started to develop an independent department and became its first professor in 1959. Despite the initial difficulties of working on two sites, he recruited several young surgeons returning from war service and developed a department with an outstanding international reputation. He was interested in treating bone tumours and established a Scottish Bone Tumour Registry, and developed links in bioengineering with Strathclyde University. He was president of the British Orthopaedic Association in 1968. Predeceased by a son (also an orthopaedic surgeon), he leaves a wife, Mary, and two daughters.

Jonathan Joseph Bolger

Former consultant in clinical oncology Sheffield, 1983-98 (b 1947; q The London 1972; FRCR), died suddenly at his desk at Weston Park Hospital on 16 December 1998. He first studied dentistry and then transferred to medicine. He was a devoted Londoner where he worked from 1972 to 1983 before becoming a consultant. An evangelist for large scale clinic trials, he was for many years on the Medical Research Council lung and bladder cancer working parties, and established a national reputation in managing urological malignancy. He retained a fierce, academic rigour from his schooldays with the Jesuits. His weekly teaching sessions were attended by colleagues and juniors who enjoyed the mixture of erudition, entertainment, and sheer fun. Jonathan was an eccentric with obscure interests. He had a huge collection of fine fountain pens and did not approve of ballpoints. His general knowledge was wide, spanning modern culture and an enthusiasm for medical history. His wardrobe was purchased from Saville Row and Oxfam. He leaves a wife, Sue, and three sons.

Douglas Victor Vaughan Bowen-Jones

Former general practitioner Bethesda, north Wales, 1948-70 (b 1910; q St Mary’s 1940), died from a stroke on 26 November 1998. The son of a general surgeon who became the local GP, Douglas chose St Mary’s with its strong rugby connection. The game was his lifelong passion; he played for St Mary’s, United Hospitals, Middlesex County, and London Welsh. He served with the Royal Navy on Atlantic and Malta convoys and tank landing craft on D Day. In general practice he enjoyed obstetrics and supervised home deliveries in what were often less than ideal surroundings, including the safe delivery of twins by an elderly primigravida, who would not consider a hospital confinement. In retirement he enjoyed gardening and beekeeping. Predeceased by his first wife, Glenys, he leaves a second wife, Freda; a daughter (also a doctor); a son; and five grandchildren.

by Ann Bowen-Jones

Mary Forbes Brownlie

Former consultant chest physician Southampton (b Scotland 1912; q Glasgow 1937; DPH), d 14 November 1998. She served in the Royal Army Medical Corps and was posted to the Isle of Wight during the expected invasion period of 1941. After demobilisation she decided to specialise in the treatment of tuberculosis and other chest diseases and worked in north Wales and Brighton before moving to Southampton. Mary went to endless trouble to help her patients in many ways other than medical. She was active in the Soroptimists club, attending many international meetings, the British Association of University Women, and the local BMA. She also worked tirelessly for charity, particularly the Save the Children Fund and cancer research, and spent hours flag selling for any charity of which she approved. Mary was unmarried.

Edgar Austin Peter Croydon

Medical director Beecham Pharmaceuticals, European Division (b Hitchin, Hertfordshire, 1926; q St George’s 1955; FFPM), died peacefully from carcinomatosis on 12 January 1999. After house posts in London Peter joined the Royal Canadian Air Force Hospitals in France as a surgeon. He joined Beecham Research Laboratories as medical adviser in 1960, and was closely involved with the emerging semisynthetic penicillin research and subsequent developments. After he retired as medical director he continued as consultant to SmithKline Beecham until he died. Peter never lost his interest in general practice and took every opportunity to deputise for his friends. He had an abiding interest in military history, particularly of the first world war, and those who visited the battlefields with him benefitted from his extensive knowledge. He was fluent in French and bought a holiday home in France. He leaves a wife, Cynthia; a daughter; and three sons.

John Lewis Francis

Consultant general physician, cardiologist, and director of nuclear medicine Stafford General Hospital (b 1934; q UCH 1958 (gold medal in medicine)), d 19 November 1998. He came to Stafford in 1971, and the nuclear medicine department, which he set up, developed into a nationally respected unit. He also developed a successful department in non-invasive cardiology. John was involved in the management of the hospital and was medical director from 1995 until his death; he was due to retire in 1999. He loved the sea and sailing his yacht Malimbe. He leaves a wife, Clare, and three sons.

Herbert Frood

Former general practitioner Chester (b Colwyn Bay 1930; q Liverpool 1957), died from malignant melanoma on 1 January 1999. The son of the founder of the Ferodo Company, Herbert first embarked on training to become a chartered surveyor and retained an enthusiasm for buildings, bridges, and railways. When he entered general practice his determination resulted in one of the first purpose built health centres in Chester, which received a Civic Trust award. He forged links with consultant colleagues and was an enthusiastic teacher. In 1991 he was invested a serving brother of the Most Venerable Order of the Hospice of St John of Jerusalem in recognition of his service as lecturer and examiner on first aid to British Rail staff. After retirement he continued to garden and devote time to his church. He served as medical officer to Cheshire Army Cadets and, as Major Frood, was regularly on duty. He was chairman of the local day care centre and provided transport for the centre and the local hospice, and at Easter 1998 he acted as medical officer to a party of pilgrims to Lourdes. He leaves a wife, Elisabeth; two sons and a daughter; and four granddaughters.

William Stevenson Hamilton

Former chest physician Oxford (b 1921; q Oxford 1944; FRCP), d 15 September 1998 after a second coronary bypass operation. Bill served in the Royal Army Medical Corps in the Middle East for four years. He worked in chest medicine all his life, witnessing the miraculous cures brought by antimicrobial treatment for tuberculosis and then moving on to cancer, but he retained a broad outlook that enabled him to spot the non-thoracic causes of respiratory symptoms. Bill was sensitive to the social pressures that impacted on progress and prognosis and ran a multidisciplinary care team long before the term was invented. His straight face kept his staff on their toes, but his flowing beard and twinkling eyes endeared him to his patients. He retired early in 1978, severely afflicted with arthritis due to haemochromatosis and ischaemic heart disease, but he pursued a wide range of activities. His Scottish pipes were heard at many festive events, and he was an accomplished painter, carver of wooden flotsam, and a potter. He leaves a wife, Ann (also a retired doctor); three children (all in the healthcare professions); and eight grandchildren.

Alexander Fordyce Johnstone

Former consultant psychiatrist Craig Dunain Hospital, Inverness, 1957-85 (b 1925; q Aberdeen 1947; FRCPsych, DPM), d 15 December 1998. He served in the Royal Air Force during the Berlin airlift. A year in general practice preceded his psychiatric career. After junior posts he moved to the Highlands and Islands, which proved a challenging venue for Fordyce’s gift of clinical acumen combined with his approachability. The rigours of travelling to the developing outpatient clinics did not diminish his cheerfulness and helpfulness. His second opinions, which were often sought, were given without pretension or condescension. Outside medicine his interests were gardening, golf, and painting. He leaves a wife, Moyra; three children; and five grandchildren.

by Martin Whittet

John Wingrave Landells

Former professor of pathology Addis Ababa, 1976-94 (b 1911; q Cambridge/The London 1936; OBE), d 3 November 1998. He started off studying classics but switched to medicine, and at medical school was scrum half playing in the interhospitals cup tie. He joined up in 1939 and was sent to France as regimental medial officer. John was mentioned in dispatches for his work during the retreat to Dunkirk where he barely escaped with his life. He also served in north Africa and Italy. On return to civilian life he started his career in morbid anatomy at The London Hospital, becoming lecturer, senior lecturer, and reader. Within two weeks of retirement in 1978, John was appointed to the chair of pathology in the faculty of medicine at the University of Addis Ababa, which had been closed for two years after the revolution. He spent 18 years building up a department from scratch, training staff and technicians, and teaching his students as he had done in London. He arranged scholarships overseas for many of his postgraduate students. One said that he showed students that “pathology was a science to be loved and if done properly could save many lives.” He was awarded the OBE “for services to medical education overseas.” John always maintained an interest in music and natural history, particularly ornithology. After giving up playing rugby football, he became a licensed referee. He leaves a wife, Norah, and three sons (one a consultant pathologist).
