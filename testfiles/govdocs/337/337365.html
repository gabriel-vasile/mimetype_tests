Seabird Picture of the Month, March 2008:
 Bridled Tern
photo by Glen Tepke

The Bridled Tern (Sterna anaethetus) ranges throughout tropical and subtropical latitudes of the Atlantic Ocean. They are present in North America along the east coast of the United States, including the Gulf of Mexico. While Bridled Terns sometimes nest in coastal mainland habitats, this species usually nests on islands, such as the West Indies, that are miles from the closest continent. Bridled Tern colonies are smaller than those belonging to other terns, and may contain only a few pairs of mating birds dispersed widely over suitable habitat. Bridled Terns don’t usually flock together, but when they do, a group is referred to as a “ternery” or a “U” of terns.

Bridled Terns frequently associate with collections of marine debris floating on the ocean’s surface called flotsam. One component of the flotsam is pelagic Sargassum, a type of algae grows and floats at the surface of the ocean. This in turn creates a habitat for the fish that the Bridled Tern eats. The Bridled Tern’s scientific species name anaethetus takes root in Greek, meaning stupid or without sense, and refers to the ease with which sailors could easily catch the birds for food. Bridled Terns have many common names as well. In the Eastern Hemisphere they are called “Dog Tern” after one of their calls that resembles a dog barking.

Source:
