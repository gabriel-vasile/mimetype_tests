ARTIFICIAL INDUCTION OF LACTATION TO EXTEND HERD LIFE IN CATTLE
 
PROJECT DIRECTOR: Barnes, M. A.
 
PERFORMING ORGANIZATION
DAIRY SCIENCE
VIRGINIA POLYTECHNIC INSTITUTE

NON TECHNICAL SUMMARY: Thirty percent of lactating dairy cattle are culled annually, many because of breeding failure and failure to initiate a new lactation. This project will investigate various protocols to artificially induce lactation in nonbreeder dairy cattle, thereby extending productive life.
 
OBJECTIVES: 1. Investigate various protocols to induce lactation in non-breeder dairy cattle in order to extend herd life. a. Determine the feasability of synchronizing estrous cycles to improve response to lactation induction. b. Determine the effect of using recombinant bovine somatotropin to improve milk yield after lactation induction. c. Determine the effect of using recombinant bovine placental lactogen to improve response to lactation induction. d. Determine the effect of using adrenocorticotropic hormone to replace dexamethasone in the lactation induction protocol.
 
APPROACH: We will utilize nonpregnant heifers and parous cows per year to investigate various protocols to induce lactation and extend herd life. Attempts will be made to establish pregnancy in those cattle that successfully initiate an induced lactation. The various objectives will be accomplished in a succession of trials utilizing 40 nonbreeder heifers and 40 nonbreeder cows per year. The control group animals for the first trial will receive the presently accepted protocol for artificially inducing lactation (exogenous progesterone and estrogen followed by exogenous reserpine and dexamethasone). Successive trials to accomplish the remaining objectives will utilize the most successful previous protocol as a control treatment. a. In the first trial, prostaglandin will be used to synchronize estrous cycles to reduce the suppressing effect of progesterone during artificial induction of lactation. b. In the second trial, recombinant bovine somatatropin will be utilized to maximize milk yield in response to induction of lactation. c. In the third trial, recombinant bovine placental lactogen will be administered during the lactation induction protocol to improve number of animals responding. d. In the fourth trial, adrenocorticotropic hormone will be administered in place of dexamethasone to reduce the need to discard milk early in lactation from cows induced into lactation. Animal response to the induction protocol in each trial will be compared to response of control animals to determine the most successful method re-establishing lactation in nonbreeders.

PROGRESS: Oct 1, 1999 TO Sep 30, 2004
Thirty-four nonbreeder, non-lactating cows (8 Jerseys and 26 Holsteins) were utilized to determine whether lactation induction response can be improved by synchronization of estrous cycles prior to initiation of lactation induction, and induced luteolysis at the time of lactogenesis. Lactation-induced (treated) animals were paired by breed and lactation number with pregnant, non-lactating (control group). Treatment group cows received 25 mg PGF2a 11 days apart prior to beginning lactation-induction protocol. Control animals received no PGF2a. All animals received .05mg/kg BW estradiol-17B and .25 mg/kg BW progesterone per day on days 1-7, followed by 25 mg PGF2a on day 14, then 20 mg dexamethasone and 5 mg reserpine on days 15-18. Beginning on day 20, all animals were milked twice daily for 150 days. Artificial induction of lactation yielded 92 percent success rate for Holsteins and 88 percent success rate for Jerseys. Mean total milk yield for induced cows was 65 percent of yields of control cows. Mean peak yields were 32 kg/d and 20 kg/d, respectively, for Holsteins and Jerseys. Mean serum and milk progesterone concentrations during the first 6 d of lactation were not different between induced and control cows. Mean serum estradiol concentration was higher in induced cows compared to control cows at 3 and 5 DIM. Lactation induced cows had elevated (260 ng/ml) serum alpha-lactalbumin concentrations 2 d prior to initiation of milking. Mean days-to-first service was reduced in induced cows compared to control cows, while mean services per conception were similar. Mean days to conception was also lower in induced cows than for control cows. Pregnancy rate by 150 DIM was 70 percent for lactation-induced cows and 56 percent for control cows.

IMPACT: 1999-10-01 TO 2004-09-30 This project was designed to investigate methods to improve success rates of artificial induction of lactation in dairy cows. It is expected that successful induction of lactation in dairy herds can reduce numbers of cows culled because of failure to breed and provide additional breeding opportunity for cows after successful induction of a new lactation.
