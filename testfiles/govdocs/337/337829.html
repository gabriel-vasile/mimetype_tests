Resilience – Skill to Cope with Stress

In conjunction with the World Mental Health Day on October 10, Ruth Imershein, RMO/P submitted the article “People Can Learn Markers on Road to Resilience,” by David Milne with text in full at this link - <http://pn.psychiatryonline.org/cgi/content/full/42/2/5?eaf>

Mr. Milne presents results of three studies of psychological characteristics of individuals who suffered severe trauma but who did not develop problems with the expected levels of depression or PTSD.  Some people even emerged from their experiences with greater strength and resilience.  It appears that stress inoculation or resilience in coping with stress can be learned through focused training and cognitive behavioral therapy. 

These are the traits that may be key to enhancing one’s resilience to major stressors:

·      Be optimistic.

·      Be able to restructure knowledge in adaptive ways (cognitive flexibility).  For example, learn what is controllable and focus on it; learn what is uncontrollable and plan how to handle it.

·      Develop a “shatterproof” set of beliefs through religion or other spirituality.

·      Be altruistic.   A mission of helping others helps oneself.

·      Be inspired by a resilient role model mentor or heroic figure. Imitation is a great way to learn.

·      Face fear and use it as a guide.  Fear is normal. Plan contingencies for worst-case scenarios.

·      Be adaptive to stress.  Work through a threat rather than resisting it.

·      Establish close and meaningful relationships that can provide emotional strength.

·      Exercise and keep fit, which keep the brain healthy.

·      Laugh frequently and enjoy a sense of humor.

The culmination of training that engages emotions, intellect, morals, physical endurance, and challenges develops resilience.  It fosters not only mental coping but also mental strengthening.  Additionally a keen sense of self-awareness, assumption of responsibility, and special helpful relationships are major factors in resilience building.
