Lesson 2A: Wind-Driven Circulation

Estimated Time: One forty-five minute class period

 

Indicator(s) Core Learning Goal 1:

Indicator(s) Core Learning Goal 2:

Student Outcome(s):

1.     The student will be able to explain how horizontal currents are generated by observing the effect of wind energy on the movement of water. 

2.     The student will be able to predict the location of surface currents by analyzing global wind patterns.

Brief Description:

This lesson builds on the concepts of global winds, which was developed in Unit II. It will help set the stage for students start to think about the interactions between the atmosphere and the hydrosphere and ever present role of energy in earth systems. Students will construct first hand knowledge of wind-driven currents by conducting a simulation. Students then apply that knowledge to complete a graphic of global wind patterns and develop a system diagram to summarize some components. Students will be asked to include the concept of the Coriolis effect in their construct, first introduced in middle school and revisited in Unit II.

 

Background knowledge / teacher notes:

 

Winds from the atmosphere are the force behind surface ocean currents. Currents are due to the force of the winds, the Earth's rotation, and the land masses that block current motions. When a wind blows across the surface of the ocean, energy is transferred from the wind to the sea surface. Winds blow across the surface of the water and drag the water along the surface. This energy is used to generate surface waves and currents. The direction of the currents is affected by the spinning of the Earth, Coriolis effect. Coriolis effect and boundaries of the shorelines cause surface currents to flow in a circular pattern. The currents exhibit the pattern of curving to the right in the Northern Hemisphere and the left in the Southern Hemisphere.

 

 

Lesson Description:  Lesson 2A

 

ENGAGE

Have students view a video of the storm surge caused by a hurricane.

Ask students to predict the effect of wind on the movement of water on the Earth's surface and think about what other factors may be involved.

EXPLORE

At their lab stations, students will observe the effects of wind on the movement of water by blowing through a straw over a shallow tray filled with water. 

Observations should include:

·       water moves horizontally and in a single direction

·       direction of water movement is determined by the wind

·       wind energy is transferred to the surface of the water creating waves and generating a current

·       speed of current is directly proportional to wind speed.

Journal Write: Students will record their observations.

EXPLAIN

In small groups, students will discuss the effects of wind on the movement of water.  Based on their observations, students will construct a definition of a current.

 

Education Elements:

 

BACKGROUND, LINKS, STUDENT ACTIVITY

This site provides an activity, information and links to explain the Coriolis force.

http://coolspace.gsfc.nasa.gov/outreach/activity/coriolis.html

 

EXTEND

On a world map worksheet, students will use arrows to indicate the global winds.  Based on the relationship among winds, Coriolis force, and surface currents, students will draw arrows to predict the location of wind-driven currents in the Northern Hemisphere.  Students should explain how landmasses influence the direction and movements of currents.  If students have difficulty with this concept, they should return to their lab station and observe the effect of the container walls on the direction and speed of a current.

Using a map provided by the teacher, students should compare their predicted ocean currents to known ocean currents.  Students should conclude that ocean surface currents coincide with the locations of global winds forming the five major gyres.

EVALUATE

Students will draw the location of wind-driven currents in the Southern Hemisphere.

Journal Write: explain what factors are used to construct wind-driven currents. Draw a systems diagram to illustrate factors that contribute to the formation of wind-driven currents.
