Personal Research Areas:

Genetic analysis and therapy development for human retinal and macular dystrophies.
Retinal electrophysiology and origins of the electroretinogram response.

I am a neurophysiologist and study the pathophysiology of genetically inherited human hereditary retinal and macular dystrophies. I also work in the field of retinal electrophysiology and neurophysiology in rodents and in non-human and human primate. The impetus for my work comes through clinical studies of human genetic retinal disorders, evaluated using non-invasive visual psychophysics and electroretinogram (ERG) recordings. The dystrophies we have studied thus far have a Mendelian genetic basis, and I incorporate genetic studies of these conditions. I have been fortunate to be involved in cloning of novel genes and in characterizing novel human phenotypes. In some cases we have replicated the dysfunction found in human phenotypes through intravitreal application of glutamate analogues in the intact monkey eye to suppress function of particular neural channels in the non-human primate retina. This latter approach has been quite successful in teasing apart the complex origins of the primate electroretinogram response.

1.) I study human genetic retinal neurodegeneration and rodent models of these conditions. We currently are studying rhodopsin mutant rodent models that we engineered and that we acquired from other laboratories. These represent a spectrum of disease mechanisms. As one example of novel information that has come from this approach, I uncovered an extensive family with autosomal dominant night-blindness but without apparent retinal degeneration. We subsequently identified a rhodopsin G90D mutation in this family, and we proposed that the night-blindness resulted from thermal instability, or constitutive activity, of rhodopsin leading to increased "dark-light noise" that desensitizes the photoreceptor cell, independent of environmental light and separate from overt rod cell degeneration. This would be one of the very few extant examples of a constitutively active G-protein coupled receptor (i.e. opsin) causing a disease state. To test the hypothesis of constitutive activity, we created a rhodopsin G90D transgenic mouse and crossed this onto a rhodopsin null background. Analysis of these G90D rodents provided solid in vivo evidence of this mechanism. We are now using these rhodopsin mutant rodent models, along with the RCS rat and the photoreceptor light damaged rat, to study pharmacologic factors that may convey neuroprotection to retinal neurons.

Sieving PA, Richards JE, Naarendorp F, Bingham EL, Scott K, Alpern M. Dark-light: Model for nightblindness from the human rhodopsin Gly90 -->Asp mutation. Proc Natl Acad Sci USA 92:880-884 (1995).

Sieving PA, Fowler ML, Bush RA, Machida S, Calvert PD, Green DG, Makino C, McHenry C. Constitutive "light" adaptation in rods from G90D rhodopsin: A mechanism for human congenital nightblindness without rod cell loss. J Neurosci 21:5449-5460 (2001).

Dizhoor AM, Woodruff ML, Olshevskaya EV, Cilluffo MC, Cornwall MC, Sieving PA, Fain GL. Night blindness and the mechanism of constitutive signaling of mutant G90D rhodopsin. J Neurosci 28(45):11662-72 (2008).

2.) We are looking at retinal diseases that result from abnormal vitamin A processing in the retinal pigment epithelium (RPE) which lies immediately adjacent to the photoreceptors. In parallel studies, we found that 13-cis-retinoic acid (Accutane) applied in vivo blocks the 11-cis-retinal dehydrogenase (RDH5) enzyme that is a final metabolic step in reconverting bleached retinoid to 11-cis-retinal in the RPE. We showed that blocking RDH5 with 13-cis-retinoic acid conveyed significant protection against acute light damage injury to photoreceptors, indicating that it is possible to target the retinoid cycling within the RPE by exogenous agents and thereby protect photoreceptors. We hypothesized that this may have application to conditions in which accumulation of A2E/lipofuscin is associated with RPE cell death, as in Stargardt's diseases and in some forms of age-related macular degeneration. Studies to test this idea are underway.

Sieving PA, Chaudhry P, Kondo M, Provenzano M, Wu D, Carlson TJ, Bush RA, Thompson DA. Inhibition of the visual cycle in vivo by 13-cis retinoic acid protects from light damage and provides a mechanism for night-blindness in isotretinoin therapy. Proc Natl Acad Sci (USA) 98:1835-1840 (2001).

Radu RA, Mata NL, Nusinowitz S, Liu X, Sieving PA, Travis GH. Treatment with isotretinoin inhibits lipofuscin accumulation in a mouse model of recessive Stargardt's macular degeneration. Proc Natl Acad Sci (USA) 100: 4742-4747 (2003).

3.) Human juvenile X-linked retinoschisis (XLRS) has occupied a considerable portion of my attention over the past decade. We pursued mapping and cloning of the XLRS gene as part of the European RS Consortium for this effort. The typical course of human XLRS disease causes reduced acuity in childhood and loss of inner retinal ERG signaling prior to the delamination of the neural retina layers which ultimately destroys the retinal integrity. XLRS may well afford a good target for human gene therapy. Since the XLRS1 protein is extracellular, delivery may prove less complicated than for other retinal dystrophies that involve integral cellular membrane proteins, such as in the photoreceptors. We have generated a transgenic mouse model of XLRS and have treated this successfully to restore retinal function by AAV gene transfer. We are now preparing to design a human clinical trial to explore safety and efficacy of gene transfer therapy.

The Center for Retinal and Macular Degenerations, W.K. Kellogg Eye Center, University of Michigan Paul A. Sieving, M.D., Ph.D., Founding Director. 1990 - 2001.

In 1990 I initiated the Center for Retinal and Macular Degenerations at the University of Michigan to facilitate investigations into causes and treatments for human inherited retinal dystrophies. Development of the Center was championed by Paul R. Lichter, MD, Chair of the Department of Ophthalmology and Visual Science. Support came from the Department of Ophthalmology and from the Medical School, with additional funding from private and foundation philanthropy. By 2001 the Center housed four laboratories and a clinical studies unit occupying approximately 5,000 sqft in the W. K. Kellogg Eye Center. Additional participating faculty had laboratory space within the Eye Center and the University. Human clinical phenotype studies were conducted in adjacent medical space. Investigators were supported in the main by NIH individual R01 grants, with additional and important support through endowment funds allocated to the Center.

The Center emphasized cross-disciplinary studies of retinal function and disease mechanisms in human patients and rodent disease models. Areas of study included molecular genetics, retinal cell and molecular biology, neurophysiology, and histopathology of natural and engineered animal models of retinal degeneration. Human disease phenotypes were investigated by clinical observation, visual psychophysics and retinal electrophysiology. Considerable effort was focused on elucidating the cellular origins of electroretinogram (ERG) components in non-human primates, to facilitate interpretation of aberrant responses encountered in human retinal dystrophies.

The Center was designed around an integrated approach to clinical and basic questions. Patients with retinal and macular dystrophies were examined on both fee-for-service and research bases, and genetic samples were collected for mapping, cloning and mutation screening. Two transgenic mouse models were engineered by Center scientists as disease specific homologs of human conditions. Pharmacologic treatment strategies were a particular focus, with the ultimate aim of evaluating promising leads in human treatment trials.

The following work was conducted as an investigator within the Center:

  • Identified a novel mechanism of night-blindness that results from constitutive activation of rhodopsin from a human G90D mutation. This constitutive activity causes an instability in binding the 11-cis-retinal chromophore and initiates the phototransduction cascade inappropriately.
  • The first identification of a contribution of hyperpolarizing bipolar cells to the primate ERG, and demonstrated signaling abnormalities of the retinal On- and Off-pathways in human rod and cone degenerations.
  • Created a G90D rhodopsin transgenic/null-background mouse model and demonstrated fidelity to the human night-blindness phenotype with no rod degeneration.
  • Studied in the P23H rhodopsin transgenic rat a possible cellular mechanism contributing to rod degeneration in this gene class of disease.
  • Established a CLIA-certified clinical molecular genotyping service for diagnosis and carrier identification.
  • Genotyped 125 human families with juvenile X-linked retinoschisis (XLRS) and provided a hypothesis of genotype-phenotype correlation.
  • Showed pharmacologic rescue of photoreceptor light-damage degeneration and the genetic RCS rat models using agents in the dopamine/melatonin pathway.
  • Identified a pharmacological approach to interrupting retinoid cycling in the retinal pigment epithelium, with work underway to explore this as a treatment strategy to reduce accumulation of A2E/lipofuscin in the Stargardt knockout mouse.
  • Performed phenotype and genetic mapping/mutation studies of rare human retinal dystrophies, including blue cone monochromacy causing macular degeneration, a non-TIMP3 form of autosomal dominant hemorrhagic macular dystrophy, and cone-rod homeobox (CRX) gene mutations causing Leber's congenital amaurosis and cone-rod dystrophy.
  • Identified and mapped to chromosome 6q an autosomal dominant family with atrophic macular degeneration and participated in cloning the ELOVL4 gene responsible.
  • Developed a retinoschisis XLRS1 gene knockout mouse model. The founder animals showed the characteristic ERG signature of b-wave loss but with preserved a-wave.We have now created a stable XLRS mouse line for study and have rescued the physiological deficit by gene transfer with an AAV vector.
