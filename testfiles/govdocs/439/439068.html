BI-PARTISAN BILL BOOSTS ANTI-BEETLE FUNDING BY $19 MILLION

 

 

New York City – Responding to the first discovery of Asian Longhorned Beetle (ALB) larvae in Central Park in three years, Rep. Anthony Weiner (D-Queens & Brooklyn) succeeded in passing legislation in the House to increase federal funds to eradicate the beetle by $19 million.

 

New York City has been fighting the ALB since it was first discovered in the United States, in Greenpoint, Brooklyn in 1996.  Since then, more than 6,000 trees in New York have been infested.

 

We know how to beat back the beetle--through a combination of tree quarantine, the removal of infected trees, and the inoculation of those that are at risk.  But to do it we need adequate funding.  The FY '06 Bush budget called for a $30 million national funding cut for Asian Longhorned Beetle, from $45 million to $15 million.  The agriculture appropriations bill brought to the floor by House Republicans contained Bush's cut, pegging funds at only $15 million for eradicating the Beetle.

 

The Weiner amendment, which he introduced with Rep. Thaddeus McCotter (R-MI), restores $19 million in funding to fight the beetle and other destructive pests, bringing funding back up to $34 million.  That's approximately the level the Animal Plant Health Inspection Service says it needs to control the beetle's spread, and eradicate it entirely by 2018. The Weiner/McCotter amendment passed yesterday afternoon as part of the agriculture appropriations bill.

 

 "In New York City's battle against the beetle, President Bush has been standing with the bugs," said Rep. Weiner. "Under this President more beetles has usually meant less money for New York.  Today funding will help go a long way to ensuring that a tree will still grow in Brooklyn, and throughout the five boroughs."

 

The ALB is an insect from China that destroys hardwood trees by disrupting the flow of nutrients and water through the trunk, roots and stem
