Twelve near-axiomatic features of slow-acting infections and transmissible agents causing them.

Smith R; International Conference on AIDS.

Int Conf AIDS. 1992 Jul 19-24; 8: 38 (abstract no. PuA 6163).

Institute for Postgraduate Interdisciplinary Studies, Palo Alto, CA 94306.

PROBLEM/ISSUES/OBJECTIVES: Lentiviruses and other agents of slow-acting infections are unique in their transmission dynamics, epidemiology and pathogenic mechanisms. Particularly intriguing are their slowly progressive features and variable incubation periods. This study identifies 'near-axiomatic' and immutable features of the agents of slow-acting infections that distinguish them from other infectious agents. PROJECT DESCRIPTION/METHODS: Ethnomethodological analyses, mathematical models, computer simulations, statistical analyses, genetic analyses, historical surveys, viral titration studies, and reviews of other available (published and unpublished) reports are used in this ongoing (13+ year) study of 'unconventional' agents of slow-acting infections (associated with scrapie, kuru, bovine spongiform encephalopathy, Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease, Gerstmann-Straussler-Scheinker syndrome, Naegleria ameba cytopathic material) and lentiviruses (i.e., subclasses of retroviruses associated with visna, maedi, caprine arthritis-encephalitis, jaagziekte, equine infectious anemia, human acquired immunodeficiency syndrome, simian acquired immunodeficiency syndrome, and bovine acquired immunodeficiency syndrome). RESULTS: If the term 'near axiomatic' is used to connote necessary, immutable and consistent features of all agents of slow-acting infections in most contexts, then twelve 'near axiomatic' features of unconventional agents and lentiviruses now have been identified. CONCLUSIONS/LESSONS LEARNED: 1) Systematic confirmation of these twelve features in rigorously controlled experimental studies is desirable, but may not be necessary or sufficient. 2) In HIV-related diseases, an inverse correlation between titers of HIV and incubation periods (i.e., dose-dependent variable incubation periods) can explain Pattern I and Pattern II distributions of HIV-related diseases. 3) As a class, agents of slow-acting infections appear to behave in a recessive functioning manner with all other transmissible agents behaving in a dominant functioning manner. Recessive function may explain the slowness of these agents and appears to be more inclusive than the genetic notion of epistasis. 4) Multivalent vaccines against opportunistic pathogens, when coupled with comprehensive educational approaches, offer great hope for controlling the pandemic of HIV-related diseases. 5) It is crucial that one distinguish between the origin and evolution of HIV and the onset and progression to AIDS and other HIV-related conditions.
