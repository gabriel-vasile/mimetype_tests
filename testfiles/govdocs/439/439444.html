Anophthalmia Induced in Rats by Pre- and Postnatal Exposure to Indinavir and Ritonavir.

RIECKE K, BAUMANN-WILSCHKE I, STAHLMANN R; Interscience Conference on Antimicrobial Agents and Chemotherapy.

Abstr Intersci Conf Antimicrob Agents Chemother Intersci Conf Antimicrob Agents Chemother. 1999 Sep 26-29; 39: 39 (abstract no. 1762).

Inst. of Clin. Pharmacology and Toxicology, Freie Univ. Berlin, Berlin, GERMANY.

BACKGROUND: Studies on the reproductive toxicity of HIV-protease inhibitors have not been published and guidelines for antiretroviral treatment of pregnant women are conflicting. We studied the developmental toxicity of indinavir and ritonavir in rats.METHODS: Pregnant Wistar rats were treated once daily with either indinavir (500 mg/kg body weight, n=20) or ritonavir (500 mg/kg body weight, n=20) or vehicle (starch solution, n=19) from day 9 of pregnancy until the end of the lactation period. Developmental landmarks were recorded.RESULTS: Treatment with ritonavir was terminated after 7 days (day 16 of pregnancy) because of maternal toxicity. Treatment with indinavir was well tolerated and the number and postnatal mortality of these offspring did not differ significantly from controls. Body weight of indinavir - exposed offspring was retarded by 3 days compared to controls whilst the weight of ritonavir - exposed offspring did not differ significantly from that of controls. Also, development of fur, eyes and testicles occurred significantly later in animals exposed to indinavir, development of teeth occurred later in those exposed to ritonavir. Anophthalmia was observed in 7 of 236 (3.0%) offspring exposed to indinavir and 2 of 113 (1.8%) offspring exposed to ritonavir.CONCLUSION: After treatment of pregnant and lactating rats with indinavir and ritonavir, we observed developmental retardation. The finding of anophthalmia in about 2% of the fetuses exposed to both protease inhibitors is alarming. These findings suggest that the treatment of pregnant women with these drugs should not be encouraged.
