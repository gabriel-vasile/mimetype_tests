Synchrotron Based Studies:

Iron oxide coating formation on quartz

Recent studies of sorption of heavy metals on aquifer sediments have indicated that the bulk of metal take-up occurs not on the silicate grains, but onto thin coatings of attached Fe-Al oxides and hydroxides. Such coatings are detectable in the micron and thicker regimes, but coatings in the nm range appear to coat all surfaces of natural quartz grains. We believe this result to be a general one, and hence have set out to characterize the type of coatings on natural mineral grains, as well as the process of formation of coatings onideal quartz substrates both in the laboratory and under natural conditions.

Characterization is being done with Atomic Force Microscopy (AFM at USGS) under wet and dry conditions, by Transmission Electron Microscopy (TEM at LBL/NCEM), by Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM at USGS), and by Grazing Incidence X-ray Absorption Spectroscopy (GIXAS at SSRL). AFM and SEM allow us to see particles and surface structures in the tens of nm regime, while TEM affords molecular resolution, and GIXAS gives us atomic-scale information.

GIXAS Measurements

The unusual sensitivity and specificity of the GIXAS method is illustrated in Figure 1. The technique uses polarized synchrotron x-rays striking a smooth sample surface just below the critical angle for total external reflection of x-rays at the wavelength chosen. Under these conditions the wavefield of the incident beam sets up a standing electric field near the sample surface that ideally sums the amplitudes of incident and reflected beams, and thus has 2x the amplitude and 4x the intensity of the x-ray electric field.

As the beam is highly polarized and extremely well-collimated, there is minimal penetration of the x-rays into the surface (e.g. 3 nm) at the critical angle, hence there is little stray x-ray scattering background. Thus the technique has exceptional signal/noise ratio and can be used to sample surface concentrations as low as 1011 atoms by detecting the excited x-ray fluorescence. In GIXAS we measure the excited fluorescence as a function of incident x-ray energy over about a 1000eV range, which yields an x-ray absorption spectrum containing both XANES and EXAFS contributions.

Besides the sensitivity, the use of polarized x-rays allow us to probe different directions in the sample surface. For example, with a suitable substrate we can detect the atomic positions normal to the surface, and in two orthogonal directions along the surface.

Quartz substrates were obtained with high quality r (10-11) and m(10-10) surface planes. These planes are the most common on natural quartz crystals, and represent the highest stability surfaces. Figure 2 shows an idealized drawing of a quartz crystal with these faces indicated. Note that the "bow-tie" configuration of faces on the left side of the crystal is indicative of the handedness of the crystal.

The sorption experiment was done by exposing the substrates to a 10-5 M Fe3+ solution for several hours, then washing off the wafers with deionized water. Figure 3 shows the type of result produced from the GIXAS experiments. This is a pair correlation function (PCF) produced by extracting the EXAFS from the raw X-ray absorption data and taking the Fourier transform with respect to the EXAFS scattering vector, k. The PCF shows the number and types of atoms around the absorbing atom, in this case Fe. The data were collected for different directions with respect to the quartz substrate by varying the sample orientation with respect to the x-ray electric field vector.

To understand this result we simulated a number of types of possible surface clusters and sorbed complexes of different sixe and orientation. Figure 4 shows the type of PCFs that would be expected from small hematite crystals sitting on the quartz surface. The directions noted are with respect to the hematite crystallographic axes. We see that the [0001] orinetation is quite distinct from the [10-10] orientation, and both differ considerably from a random orientation (isotropic) case.

Figure 5 shows simulations of PCFs for several types of small hematite-like clusters. The 47 and 21 atom clusters show how the PCF would look only for the central Fe atom. As the peripheral Fe atoms of the cluster would have smaller numbers of adjacent neighbors, a weighted average over all Fe sites for a given cluster must be used. Such a calculation for a 21 atom cluster shows good agreement with the PCF taken normal to the quartz r-plane surface, and hence that the [0001] direction in the microhematite clusters has this general orientation. Other work has verified the "in-plane" [10-10] orientation. The fact that the precipitates are textured but not affected by type of quartz plane suggests that they are not epitaxial, but rather controlled by larger scale surface features or interfacial effects.

Figure 6 shows a portion of the hematite structure with the [0001] direction represented. The middle layer of polyhedra has been largely removed to show the relationship of each layer and of each polyhedron to others. In hematite each FeO6 polyhedron shares faces with one other such polyhedron, edges with three polyhedra, and corners with nine polyhedra. A 21 atom cluster would consist of only about 4 Fe atoms with surrounding oxygen coordination polyhedra.

Zn precipitation and sorption
on ferrihydrite

Ferrihydrite is a ubiquitous hydrous iron hydroxide that commonly forms in acidic waters associated with abandoned mines and mine tailings. It’s usual fine grain size and hence high surface area, in addition to a strong affinity for metal sorption, make it one of the most important phases in contaminant cycling. Zinc sorption by ferrihydrite is characterized by non-Langmuir behavior at moderate sorption densities. This means that the density of sorbed zinc ions on the ferrihydrite surface does not vary linearly as a function of solution zinc concentration. Such activity suggests changes in sorbed complexes or the beginning of precipitation as sorption density rises. As the non-linear behavior occurs at lower zinc concentrations than would be consistent with precipitation of known zinc hydroxide species, there may be a surface-catalyzed precipitation process.

Zinc hydroxides and complexes with aqueous anions can form a wide variety of structures, with zinc having 4,5 or 6 coordination by oxygen or hydroxyl. Often multiple coordinations occur within the same phase. This complicated crystal chemistry make the elucidation of zinc precipitation and sorption daunting.

To examine this system we used X-ray absorption spectroscopy on the Zn and Fe K-edges. EXAFS analysis of a series of samples produced the Zn-X PCFs shown in Figures 7 and 8. The second small peak is due to Fe or Zn neighbors and is seen to vary markedly with zinc sorption density.

Interestingly, it decreases with increasing density. This indicates that as the zinc on the ferrihydrite surface increases, the sorbed zinc is attached to fewer and fewer iron atoms on the surface. The simplist interpretation is that at low zinc concentrations in coprecipitated samples (i.e. zinc added to the Fe solution from which the ferrihydrite is made), there is ample iron to complex the available zinc in a polydentate complex. As zinc concentration increases this is no longer possible, and a bidentate complex is formed. With still higher zinc concentration zinc mainly bonds directly to other sorbed zinc, and little Fe neighbors are seen. Finally, at high zinc concentrations a new kind of precipitate structure forms.

Figure 9 shows only the Zn K-edge XANES or near-edge x-ray absorption spectra of a set of coprecipitation samples with varied zinc sorption density. The spectra change with zinc density owing to variations in the structure of the complexes. The XANES spectra can be simulated using ab initio quantum codes as shown in figure 10. These simulations verify the structure of the complexes as suggested by the EXAFS analysis.
